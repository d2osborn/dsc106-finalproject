How are MLB hitters staying alive?

Baseball Savant recently released new swing metrics: attack angle (º), attack direction (º), and swing path (tilt) (º)

These new metrics allow the public to take a deeper dive into swing geometry and better understand how hitters approach each at-bat.
For example, offering insight into how a player like Mookie Betts can hit 30 home runs despite being 5'9" with below-average swing speed.
To help reveal the relationship between a hitter's swing characteristics and the outcomes they produce, we have segmented situations based on the strike counts.


It's common knowledge that hitters are taught to use a "B swing" in two-strike counts.
Do the data reveal major leaguers follow this advice?
Are they adjusting their swing geometry, when facing two strikes, or is the idea of a two-strike approach more myth than reality?
These adjustments can dramatically affect batted-ball outcomes like wOBA, contact%, and groundball%.


While many hitters attemps to adjust their swings with two strikes, Yordan Alvarez stands out.
Despite large changes in his swing direction and attack angle, he remains elite in two-strike performance (e.g., top percentile in wOBA and contact%).
His swing shows big structural changes, yet his contact quality stays high. This is uncommon.


As stated earlier, every hitter has a unique way of managing tough counts; some shorten up, some stay aggressive, and others (like Yordan) retool their entire swing shape.
