His stats on the year speak for themselves: his 0.397 wOBA fell in the 96th percentile, his 16.7 barrel% fell in the 91st percentile, his 35 HRs fell in the 94th percentile, and his 107 mph EV90 fell in the 94th percentile, across all qualifying MLB hitters. This project will investigate how he had such a successful season by looking at the quality of his contact, how he approached two-strike counts, and more.

2 Strike Contact% vs wOBA

In two strikes, hitters are taught to protect the zone by shortening up and flattening their swing to prevent a strikeout and stay alive, referred to as a “B swing.” As a result, while hitters may improve their ability to make contact, the effectiveness of that contact often declines, meaning metrics like contact% may rise, but wOBA tends to fall. Yordan’s numbers in two-strike counts tell a different story: he didn’t sacrifice the quality of his swings and still ranks among the league’s top hitters, as seen in this scatter plot. So what is he doing differently?

Yordan's 2-Strike Profile

Well Yordan’s numbers, in comparison to the rest of the league, offer insight into how he’s able to survive and be successful in two-strike counts. Looking at the radar plot, we can see his high chase% and oppo% immediately stand out. While swinging out of the zone is typically seen as a negative, Yordan is able to balance this out by hitting the ball the other way. He’s able to hit the ball against the right-side infield shift, which is a valuable trait to have in two strikes.

Contact% Between Counts

The side-by-side strike zone heatmaps reveal a significant difference in Yordan’s contact profile from zero strikes to two strikes. With no strikes, he focuses on making contact on pitches inside and over the heart of the plate, which aligns with his natural swing path. However, with two strikes, he expands the zone and makes contact mostly away from him. This not only shows his ability to adjust his approach down in the count but also that he is able to maintain strong contact quality.

Conclusion

While the scatter plots suggest some correlation between changes in swing mechanics and outcome metrics, the newly introduced swing angles offer a clearer picture: there isn’t a one-size-fits-all solution for two-strike approaches. Instead, different combinations of mechanical adjustments, such as changes in these angles individually, can benefit players in different ways. Hitters who understand the geometry of their own swing can tailor their adjustments to maintain performance in tough situations. Managers and coaches can also use these metrics to gain some analytical insights into a player’s tendencies and even optimize lineup decisions as a result. It’s important to recognize that these metrics exist within a broader context, as factors such as height, weight, pitch recognition, mental approach, and situational pressure all play critical roles in a hitter’s success in two strikes. Therefore, while these swing angles provide valuable insight into a hitter’s swing, they should be used alongside other data and qualitative evaluation to form a more complete understanding of two-strike hitting.
